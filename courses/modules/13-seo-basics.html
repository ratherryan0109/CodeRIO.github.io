SEO Basics

Beginner 45 min Module 13 of 14

Learning Objectives

  • Understand what SEO is and how search engines rank pages
  • Use meta tags (title, description, keywords) effectively
  • Structure your HTML for search engine crawlers
  • Learn on-page SEO techniques you can implement with HTML alone

SEO Basics Explained

Think of it like this:

Imagine your website is a store in a massive mall (the internet). SEO is everything you do to make sure people can find your store. The mall directory (search engine) needs to know what you sell, where you are located, and why someone should visit. The <title> tag is like your store's sign — it tells people what the store is. The <meta description> is the blurb in the directory listing. And the heading structure is like the department signs inside your store that help people find what they need.

SEO (Search Engine Optimization) is the practice of improving your website to increase its visibility in search engines like Google, Bing, and DuckDuckGo. When someone searches for "how to build a website," Google's crawlers scan billions of pages to find the most relevant results. The better your SEO, the higher your page appears in search results — and the more traffic you get.

As an HTML developer, you have direct control over on-page SEO — factors that happen within your HTML code. This includes: the page title (what appears on the browser tab and in search results), the meta description (the snippet under the title in search results), heading hierarchy (search engines use headings to understand content structure), semantic HTML (crawlers trust <article> over <div>), image alt text (helps images appear in image search), and internal linking (helps crawlers discover your pages).

Beginner Example

<!DOCTYPE html>
<html lang="en">
<head>
  <meta charset="UTF-8">
  <meta name="viewport" content="width=device-width, initial-scale=1.0">
  <title>Best Chocolate Cake Recipe | Easy Baking Guide</title>
  <meta name="description" content="Learn how to bake a delicious chocolate cake from scratch. Step-by-step guide with ingredients, baking times, and frosting tips.">
  <meta name="robots" content="index, follow">
</head>
<body>
  <h1>How to Bake a Chocolate Cake</h1>
  <p>This guide will teach you everything you need...</p>
</body>
</html>

Line-by-Line Explanation:

  • Line 6: <title> — the most important SEO tag. Keep it under 60 characters. Include the primary keyword near the beginning. This is what appears as the blue link in search results.
  • Line 7: <meta name="description"> — the snippet that appears below the title in search results. Keep it between 150-160 characters. Make it compelling to get clicks.
  • Line 8: meta robots — tells search engines to index this page and follow its links. Use noindex for pages you do not want in search results (e.g., thank-you pages).
  • Line 10: A single <h1> that matches the page topic. The h1 should closely relate to the title tag.

Intermediate Example

<head>
  <title>10 HTML Best Practices for Beginners | Learn Web Dev</title>
  <meta name="description" content="Discover 10 essential HTML best practices every beginner should know. Improve your code quality, accessibility, and SEO with these simple tips.">
  <meta name="keywords" content="HTML best practices, web development, HTML tips">
  <link rel="canonical" href="https://example.com/html-best-practices">
  <meta property="og:title" content="10 HTML Best Practices for Beginners">
  <meta property="og:description" content="Improve your HTML with these 10 essential tips.">
  <meta property="og:image" content="https://example.com/thumb.jpg">
  <meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image">
</head>

Line-by-Line Explanation:

  • Line 4: meta keywords — once important, now largely ignored by Google. Still used by some smaller search engines.
  • Line 5: rel="canonical" — tells search engines which URL is the "official" version when you have duplicate content. Prevents SEO dilution from multiple URLs pointing to the same content.
  • Lines 6-8: Open Graph meta tags — control how your page appears when shared on Facebook, LinkedIn, and other social platforms. og:image is the thumbnail shown in the share card.
  • Line 9: Twitter Card — controls how your page appears in tweets. summary_large_image shows a large image preview.

Advanced Example

<head>
  <title>Complete SEO Guide: On-Page Optimization for Developers</title>
  <meta name="description" content="A comprehensive guide to on-page SEO for web developers. Learn meta tags, structured data, heading hierarchy, and performance optimization.">
  <script type="application/ld+json">
  {
    "@context": "https://schema.org",
    "@type": "Article",
    "headline": "Complete SEO Guide for Developers",
    "author": { "@type": "Person", "name": "Alex Rivera" },
    "datePublished": "2026-01-15",
    "image": "https://example.com/seo-guide.jpg"
  }
  </script>
</head>
<body>
  <article>
    <h1>Complete SEO Guide: On-Page Optimization</h1>
    <img src="seo-guide.jpg" alt="SEO optimization diagram showing meta tags and heading structure">
    <a href="/seo-checklist.html" title="Downloadable SEO checklist for developers">Download the SEO checklist</a>
  </article>
</body>

Line-by-Line Explanation:

  • Lines 4-12: Structured Data (JSON-LD) — a format that provides search engines with explicit information about your content. This can enable rich results like star ratings, recipe cards, and event listings in search results.
  • Line 15: Using <article> instead of <div> helps search engines identify self-contained pieces of content. Google's crawler understands semantic HTML better than generic containers.
  • Line 17: Descriptive alt text that includes relevant keywords naturally. Do not keyword-stuff — write for humans first.
  • Line 18: title attribute on links provides additional context. The link text itself is descriptive ("Download the SEO checklist" not "Click here").

Assignment

Create an SEO-optimized blog post page. The topic is your choice (e.g., "How to Learn JavaScript in 2026"). Your page must include: an optimized title tag (under 60 chars), a compelling meta description (150-160 chars), Open Graph and Twitter Card meta tags, a canonical URL, a single h1 that matches the topic, proper heading hierarchy (h2 for sections, h3 for sub-sections), at least 3 images with descriptive alt text, internal links to other pages, and structured data (JSON-LD Article schema). Test the page with Google's Rich Results Test and Lighthouse SEO audit. Save as seo-blog-post.html.

Hints:

  • Use Google's Rich Results Test (search for it) to validate your structured data.
  • Run Lighthouse in Chrome DevTools and check the SEO section for specific recommendations.
  • Keep your title unique per page — every page on your site should have a different title tag.

Interview Questions

Q: What is the difference between on-page and off-page SEO?

On-page SEO refers to factors you control within your HTML — title tags, meta descriptions, heading structure, image alt text, internal linking, and content quality. Off-page SEO refers to external factors like backlinks (other sites linking to you), social media signals, and domain authority. As an HTML developer, you primarily influence on-page SEO.

Q: Why is page speed an SEO factor?

Google considers page speed a ranking factor because users expect fast-loading pages. A slow page leads to higher bounce rates and poor user experience. Optimize images, minify CSS/JS, use lazy loading, and leverage browser caching. Tools like PageSpeed Insights can help identify performance bottlenecks.

Q: How does heading structure affect SEO?

Search engines use headings to understand the structure and hierarchy of your content. The <h1> should contain the primary keyword and describe the page topic. <h2> elements break down major sections, and <h3> elements provide sub-topics. A clear heading structure helps crawlers index your content correctly and can improve rankings for related keywords.

Common Mistakes

  • Keyword stuffing: Repeating the same keyword unnaturally many times in hopes of ranking higher. Google penalizes this. Write naturally for humans.
  • Duplicate title tags: Every page on your site should have a unique title. Duplicate titles confuse search engines and hurt rankings.
  • Missing meta descriptions: Google may auto-generate a description, but it might not be compelling. Write your own to control what users see.
  • Broken internal links: Links that lead to 404 pages waste crawl budget and frustrate users. Regularly audit your site for broken links.
